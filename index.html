Understanding Burnout

College is often described as a time of discovery and growth, but for many students it becomes a period of overwhelming stress, exhaustion, and emotional pressure. Burnout affects the body, mind, and emotions—and it often builds slowly over time.

According to national surveys, over 70% of college students experience burnout symptoms. When productivity becomes tied to self-worth, rest begins to feel like guilt.

"Burnout is what happens when you try to avoid being human for too long." – Michael Gungor

What is Causing Burnout?

Burnout develops gradually and often invisibly. College students commonly experience burnout due to overlapping pressures such as academics, finances, and comparison culture.

According to the Mayo Clinic, burnout occurs when demands exceed the resources you have to cope, resulting in emotional, physical, and mental exhaustion.

Common Causes

  • Academic Pressure: Exams, essays, and deadlines create nonstop stress.
  • Self-worth tied to performance: Grades become a measure of identity.
  • Financial stress: Working long hours while studying drains energy.
  • Comparison culture: Social media magnifies insecurities.
  • Overcommitment: Clubs, jobs, and obligations pile up.
  • Lack of rest: Sleep deprivation becomes normalized.

Strategies to Improve

Recovery is a process requiring consistent, intentional habits.

1. Work in Intervals

The Pomodoro method (25 minutes work, 5 minutes rest) has been shown to improve focus (Jung & Lee study).

2. Care for Your Mind

Blue light disrupts sleep, according to Harvard Health Publishing.

3. Care for Your Body

Exercise reduces anxiety and improves mood (NLM study).

4. Build Relationships

Isolation worsens burnout—connection improves resilience.

5. Create Gentle Structure

Use planners, scheduling apps, and realistic goals.
