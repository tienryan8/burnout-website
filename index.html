Home Page – Understanding Burnout

College should be the time where students can grow, discover, and find chances for opportunity, but it often feels for many of us like a continuous source of stress, fatigue, and pressure. With classes, part-time jobs, internships, and social obligations, emotional exhaustion easily takes over. Burnout is more than a state of tiredness but rather a condition of physical, mental, and emotional unrest resulting from prolonged or repeated stress without enough rest and recuperation for an individual.

Recent research shows that more than 70% of college students are experiencing symptoms of burnout, which include exhaustion, detachment, and loss of motivation. As success is tied in with being productive all the time, rest starts to feel like failure.

This website was created by students to help other students and people in need to identify signs of burnout, learn about healthy strategies to cope with, and find a community of support at FSU. In sharing our stories and some practical tools, we hope to remind students that balance—not perfection—is the real goal.

“Burnout is what happens when you try to avoid being human for too long.” — Michael Gungor

Page 2 – What Causes Burnout?

Burnout is rarely due to a single cause; it's usually the result of increasing pressures from a number of factors: for college students, these might include academic expectations, social comparisons, and financial hardship. As the Mayo Clinic summarizes, burnout “occurs when demands exceed the resources you have to cope with,” creating “a state of emotional, mental and physical exhaustion” and a reduced interest in activities. (Mayo Clinic Staff 2024)

Higher education—in its structure at FSU and nationally—has rewarded many individuals to overwork themselves. Long hours of studying, meals skipped, and nights awake have been normalized as symptoms of “dedication.” Those habits erode mental health and degrade learning.

We equate our self-worth with good grades, internships, and being busy all the time. Well-being and achievement are not opposites, but they can and should coexist. Setting boundaries for oneself, asking for help, and saying no when necessary are not looked down upon but should be seen as essential valuable skills.

The Cycle of Burnout:

  • High expectations can cause one to constant overwork.
  • Stress creates less rest and social time.
  • Exhaustion sets in and causes motivation to drop.
  • Self-blame increases causing one to push themselves harder.
  • Emotional detachment can lead to burnout.

Recognizing this cycle helps us break it. The next step is to learn sustainable strategies to manage it.

Page 3 – Strategies to Improve

Recovery from burnout doesn't occur overnight; it is a process of self-awareness and tiny, intentional changes consistently. Such tips are meant for university students to balance academic and personal life.

1. Take Your Time

Try the Pomodoro Method: work for 25 minutes, then take a 5-minute break. Structured micro-breaks have been shown to improve focus and reduce fatigue, according to the research of Jung and Lee (2022). Never push through being exhausted, as productivity follows rest—not the other way around.

2. Take Care of Your Mind

Digital burnout is a thing. Limit your phone usage, especially before bed, as exposure to screens before bed gives you that feeling of alertness, which can delay rest (Harvard Health Publishing 2022). Replace late-night scrolling with a short session of mindfulness, journaling, or calming music.

3. Take Care of Your Body

Physical well-being is strongly linked to one's psychological well-being. Move for joy, not punishment—take a walk around campus, stretch between classes, or even dance in your dorm room to raise one’s mood and energy. Hydration, eating real meals focused on nutrition, and getting sunlight daily helps exponentially. Findings from the National Library of Medicine indicate that moderate exercise lowers anxiety and fatigue among students (Nguyen et al. 2021).

4. Establish a Connection

Isolation can be caused by burnout and even deepen it. Reach out to trusted friends, mentors, or counselors because even one small real conversation with a trusted individual can lighten one’s emotional weight.

5. Create Structure

Use a planner or digital app to schedule breaks and self-care. Other tools, such as Notion or ThriveFSU, can help you keep track of your daily routines. Think of structure supporting your goals, not suffocating them.

Page 4 – Student Stories

Real stories remind us that burnout is not just a buzzword; it is a lived experience shared silently by many students. These accounts come from FSU students and peers from around the country who have found ways to recover and redefine success.

Jordan, Junior – “Learning to Rest Without Guilt”

“I couldn't focus, eat, or socialize. I thought there was something wrong with me, until I realized what my body was doing was protecting itself against overload. I reached out to FSU Counseling and started therapy. I'm not 'fixed,' but I'm learning that the rest is productive.”

Mia, Senior – “Balancing Two Jobs and Classes”

“Burnout felt inevitable when I was working full-time and studying full-time. What helped was finding micro-moments to reset—taking five deep breaths between shifts, walking around campus, and forgiving myself when I couldn’t do it all.”

Eli, Sophomore – “Redefining Success”

“I used to relate my personal worth with grades. When I failed my first exam, I spiraled. Talking with my professor helped me realize that the learning process isn't a linear equation that works for every student. That conversation helped me regain my perspective.”

These stories remind us that no one is alone in this struggle. Recovery is not about perfection—it's about patience.

Page 5 – Resources & Support

You don't have to go through burnout alone. These resources offer confidential, accessible help for FSU students and beyond.

Campus Resources

  • FSU Counseling & Psychological Services (CAPS): Free therapy, group sessions, and crisis care. counseling.fsu.edu
  • ThriveFSU App: Digital support for mindfulness, scheduling, and goal tracking.
  • University Health Services (UHS): Health coaching and mental wellness resources.

National Hotlines

  • 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline: Call or text 988 (24/7)
  • Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741 for free, confidential support.
  • The Trevor Project: LGBTQ+ youth helpline: 1-866-488-7386

Digital Tools

  • Headspace Student Plan: Free meditation and stress management app.
  • Notion Burnout Journal Template: Track energy, habits, and gratitude.

Remember: recovery starts with a single step. This isn’t a solo and personal journey, but one should know that support is always available.
